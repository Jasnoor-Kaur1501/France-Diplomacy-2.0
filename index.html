Motion 1 · Moderated Caucus · 90 sec · Version A — Moral clarity
Examining the conditions and status of Internally Displaced People and International Refugees globally in order to provide protection and fundamental rights to such persons
Honourable Chair — France rises because the international refugee and displacement regime was not designed for the world we are currently governing. The 1951 Convention and its 1967 Protocol were written for a post-war European context. They were written for people fleeing defined political persecution, with clear borders and functioning asylum systems. They were not written for the 117 million people forcibly displaced today — people displaced by climate events, by stateless armed groups, by prolonged conflicts with no territorial front, by governments that persecute through bureaucratic attrition rather than overt political act. The protection gap is not an accident. It is what happens when the instrument outlives the conditions that produced it. France is not here to romanticise the Convention or condemn it. It is a foundational document and it remains operative. But this committee should be honest that the Refugee Status Determination process, in its current form, excludes categories of people who are in genuine danger and who have genuine rights. The IDP framework is softer still — the Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement are not binding. They are aspirational. And when a principle is aspirational, a state can safely ignore it without consequence. France proposes that this committee direct its work toward three things: strengthened reception conditions that are rights-consistent rather than deterrence-based, formal extension of protection frameworks to climate-displaced persons, and binding host-state obligations that are tied to international funding mechanisms so that burden-sharing is not voluntary and therefore uneven. What is offered here is not idealism. It is system design. The chair is thanked.
Motion 1 · Moderated Caucus · 90 sec · Version B — Cold precision
Examining the conditions and status of Internally Displaced People and International Refugees globally
Honourable Chair — There are 117 million forcibly displaced people in the world today. Not historically. Now. This morning. Of those, approximately 43 million are refugees with formal status under the 1951 Convention. The rest exist in a legal architecture that was not designed to protect them — internally displaced persons governed by non-binding Guiding Principles, climate migrants with no recognised legal category at all, stateless persons caught in jurisdictional voids that no existing framework reaches. France wants to be precise about what this means. It means that the majority of the world's displaced population is accessing humanitarian assistance — when they access it at all — through ad-hoc arrangements, bilateral charity, and the discretionary goodwill of host governments. That is not a protection regime. That is improvisation at scale. This committee has an opportunity to address three structural failures: first, the non-refoulement principle is inconsistently applied and under-enforced, with no binding accountability mechanism when states violate it; second, burden-sharing arrangements are voluntary and therefore collapse whenever a receiving country faces domestic political pressure; third, the IDP framework has no binding teeth. France's position is not that the existing framework is worthless. It is that a framework which covers 37% of the people it was conceptually designed to protect is a framework that requires urgent structural reform. The committee's attention to this is overdue. The chair is thanked.
Motion 1 · Moderated Caucus · 90 sec · Version C — Use if someone else went first and got sentimental
Examining the conditions and status of Internally Displaced People and International Refugees globally
Honourable Chair — France appreciates the humanity in what has been said in this chamber today. But France rises to redirect the committee toward the structural, because sentiment without architecture does not protect a single person. The displacement crisis is a governance crisis. It is not a shortage of goodwill — there is considerable goodwill on display in this room right now. It is a shortage of binding obligation, enforcement mechanisms, and financial burden-sharing structures that do not collapse the moment a host country changes government. Turkey hosts 3.6 million Syrians. Lebanon, with a population of 5 million, hosts an estimated 1.5 million — the highest per-capita ratio in the world. Uganda has absorbed over a million people from South Sudan and the DRC. These are not wealthy states. They are bearing a burden that the international community has designed for them to bear because voluntary burden-sharing frameworks allow wealthy states to decide that generosity is optional. France has a direct obligation here. France receives refugees. France contributes to UNHCR. But France also acknowledges that the European asylum system has produced deterrence policies that are in tension with the Convention — and France is naming that because credibility in this chamber requires honesty about one's own record as much as critique of others'. What this committee should produce is not a resolution that expresses concern. It should produce commitments — on reception conditions, on binding burden-sharing tied to GDP contributions, on climate displacement recognition — that are operationally real. The chair is thanked.
Motion 2 · Sol Mod · 60 sec · Focusing on cooperation with UNHRC and international bodies
Focussing on the cooperation of member states with international bodies such as the UNHRC and other Global Partners
Honourable Chair — France rises to make a point that this committee should sit with. International bodies do not fail because they lack mandates. They fail because member states treat cooperation as optional when it is inconvenient. The Universal Periodic Review has been weaponised as a diplomatic performance rather than a genuine accountability mechanism. States prepare polished reports, accept recommendations selectively, and face no consequence for ignoring the rest. UNHCR field operations are chronically underfunded not because the money does not exist but because contributions are voluntary and therefore politically contingent. France's position is simple: cooperation is not a courtesy. It is the structural precondition for a functioning international human rights architecture. A state that participates in this body while systematically obstructing its field mechanisms is not a cooperating member — it is a spoiler with a seat at the table. The sol mod exists to make cooperation concrete. France is ready to work. The chair is thanked.
Motion 2 · Sol Mod · 60 sec · Alternate version — if you need to sound more collaborative
Cooperation of member states with UNHRC and Global Partners
Honourable Chair — France moves for this sol mod because the most consequential gap in the international human rights system right now is not the absence of norms. We have norms. The gap is implementation — and implementation requires cooperation. UNHCR, the Special Rapporteurs, the treaty bodies — these institutions carry the weight of the entire international human rights architecture. When states deny access, withhold reporting, or contribute funding conditionally, they are not exercising sovereignty. They are dismantling a system they signed on to build. France proposes that this working session focus on three concrete outputs: an enhanced state-reporting mechanism with independent verification, a dedicated funding stream for UNHCR field operations that is insulated from political conditionality, and a formal framework for NGO and civil society access in displacement zones. We cannot build what we will not fund, and we cannot enforce what we will not monitor. The chair is thanked.

Chair Chits — Motion 1 (IDP & Refugees)
Chair Chit M1 · 01 — Procedural clarity on IDP vs refugee framing
France respectfully draws the Chair's attention to a distinction that several delegations appear to be collapsing in debate: internally displaced persons are governed by the Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement, which are non-binding, while refugees hold status under the 1951 Convention. These are different legal instruments with different enforceability. France requests that the Chair encourage delegations to be precise about which category they are addressing to maintain the substantive rigour of debate.
Chair Chit M1 · 02 — Non-refoulement principle being mischaracterised
France notes that at least one delegation has characterised non-refoulement as a discretionary principle subject to national security carve-outs at state discretion. France submits for the Chair's awareness that Article 33(2) of the 1951 Convention provides a narrow exception for individuals who are a danger to the security of the host country — it does not provide a broad national security discretion to return individuals to persecution. France requests that this distinction be noted in the formal record of debate.

France respectfully notes that the current line of debate has shifted significantly toward bilateral political grievances unrelated to the motion's substantive scope on refugee and IDP protection conditions. Bilateral Treaty · France — Federal Republic of Germany
Franco-German Framework Agreement on Refugee Protection, Displacement Response, and International Institutional Cooperation
BILATERAL FRAMEWORK AGREEMENT Between: THE FRENCH REPUBLIC, hereinafter "France" THE FEDERAL REPUBLIC OF GERMANY, hereinafter "Germany" Collectively referred to as "the Parties" ──────────────────────────────────────────── PREAMBLE ──────────────────────────────────────────── RECALLING the longstanding Franco-German partnership as the foundational axis of European integration and shared democratic governance, REAFFIRMING the Parties' obligations under the 1951 Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees and its 1967 Protocol, the 1966 International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, and the European Convention on Human Rights, RECOGNISING that Europe hosts a disproportionate share of the global asylum-seeker population relative to its capacity to receive them under current institutional arrangements, ACKNOWLEDGING that voluntary burden-sharing mechanisms within the European Union have repeatedly failed to produce equitable distribution of protection responsibilities, NOTING that climate-induced displacement constitutes an emerging and structurally unaddressed category of forced migration for which no binding legal framework currently exists, DETERMINED to advance, within the framework of the United Nations Human Rights Council, a binding international protection regime that closes the legal and financial gaps in the current refugee and displacement architecture, HAVE AGREED AS FOLLOWS: ──────────────────────────────────────────── PART I — REFUGEE PROTECTION AND ASYLUM REFORM ──────────────────────────────────────────── ARTICLE 1 — Harmonised Reception Standards 1.1 The Parties commit to establishing harmonised minimum reception standards for asylum seekers that exceed the current EU Reception Conditions Directive, with specific attention to: access to legal representation within 48 hours of arrival; documentation issuance within 30 days; and housing conditions meeting established welfare benchmarks. 1.2 The Parties will jointly develop a model reception framework for voluntary adoption by non-EU member states, to be advanced through UNHCR technical assistance channels. ARTICLE 2 — Non-Refoulement Enforcement Mechanism 2.1 The Parties acknowledge that non-refoulement under Article 33 of the 1951 Convention is a non-derogable norm and commit to establishing a bilateral review mechanism to flag instances where third-state compliance is questionable. 2.2 Where the bilateral mechanism identifies a potential violation, the Parties will jointly raise the matter through the appropriate UNHCR or treaty body channel. ARTICLE 3 — Climate Displacement Recognition 3.1 The Parties commit to jointly advancing, in all relevant international fora including the UNHRC, the formal legal recognition of climate-displaced persons as a distinct category requiring international protection. 3.2 The Parties will support the development of a Climate Displacement Protocol to the 1951 Convention, and will co-author a model clause for consideration at the next UNHCR Executive Committee session. ──────────────────────────────────────────── PART II — BURDEN-SHARING AND INSTITUTIONAL FUNDING ──────────────────────────────────────────── ARTICLE 4 — Binding European Burden-Sharing 4.1 The Parties commit to jointly advocating for a binding EU burden-sharing mechanism based on a composite index incorporating: GDP per capita; population size; unemployment rate; and existing asylum applicant numbers. 4.2 The Parties oppose any burden-sharing arrangement that allows financial payment in lieu of physical reception, which they consider inconsistent with the non-derogable nature of international protection obligations. ARTICLE 5 — UNHCR and OHCHR Funding Floor 5.1 The Parties commit to maintaining their UNHCR voluntary contributions at or above current levels and to jointly advocating for an assessed mandatory contribution mechanism through which all UN member states contribute to UNHCR operations proportional to GDP. 5.2 The Parties will jointly table a resolution at the UNHRC supporting an increase in the OHCHR's assessed budget allocation from 0.49% to at least 1.5% of the total UN budget over a five-year period. ──────────────────────────────────────────── PART III — INTERNATIONAL BODY COOPERATION ──────────────────────────────────────────── ARTICLE 6 — UPR Implementation Verification 6.1 The Parties support the establishment of an independent verification mechanism for UPR recommendations accepted by member states, with mandatory reporting at the midpoint of each review cycle. 6.2 The Parties commit to full implementation of all UPR recommendations they have accepted and to publishing annual implementation progress reports. ARTICLE 7 — Special Rapporteur Access 7.1 The Parties extend standing invitations to all UN Special Rapporteurs and will process visit requests within 90 days of submission. 7.2 The Parties jointly advocate for a General Assembly resolution establishing that outstanding Special Rapporteur visit requests of more than two years' duration be treated as automatic standing invitations, unless the state formally objects and provides written justification. ──────────────────────────────────────────── PART IV — OPERATIONAL PROVISIONS ──────────────────────────────────────────── ARTICLE 8 — Diplomatic Coordination 8.1 The Parties will coordinate voting positions in the UNHRC on questions relating to refugee protection, IDP frameworks, and international body cooperation, and will consult prior to tabling any resolution in these areas. 8.2 The Parties will establish a bilateral working group that convenes quarterly to review progress under this Agreement and coordinate strategy in upcoming UNHRC sessions. ARTICLE 9 — Entry Into Force and Review 9.1 This Agreement enters into force upon signature by authorised representatives of both Parties. 9.2 The Agreement is subject to review every two years and may be extended, amended, or terminated by mutual written consent. ──────────────────────────────────────────── IN WITNESS WHEREOF the undersigned, duly authorised, have signed this Agreement. Strategic Intelligence
Competition Analysis
Somalia, China, Venezuela, Iran. What they'll say, how to counter, and the line that ends their argument in the room.
🇸🇴 Somalia
THREAT LEVEL: HIGH · Will be aggressive, likely to challenge Western credibility
What they'll argue

Somalia will lean hard on the hypocrisy angle — Western states closing borders while preaching protection. They'll cite European pushbacks in the Mediterranean, UK Rwanda policy, and the EU's externalisation deals with Libya and Tunisia. They'll frame refugee rights as a Global South burden while Western states write resolutions about it. They may try to advance regional solutions framed around African solidarity to lock you out of African Group support.

France's counter strategy

Don't deflect the hypocrisy charge — absorb it. "France acknowledges that European border policy has not always been consistent with the Convention framework — we are naming that rather than defending it." This takes the ammunition out of Somalia's hands. Then pivot: "But self-criticism is not the same as equivalence. Somalia has 3.8 million internally displaced citizens, no binding IDP mechanism, and Al-Shabaab territory where no UNHCR access exists. France is here to improve the international system. What is Somalia here to do?"

The line that ends it

"Pointing at France's flaws does not protect a single displaced Somali. France is proposing specific mechanisms. Somalia is proposing a deflection."

POI to have ready

"The delegation speaks to Western responsibility. France accepts that critique. Can the delegation now speak to Somalia's own responsibility — specifically, what binding protection mechanism the Somali government has established for the 3.8 million Somalis displaced within its own borders, in territory it claims sovereignty over?"

🇨🇳 China
THREAT LEVEL: VERY HIGH · Will attempt to reframe the entire agenda around economic development
What they'll argue

China will argue that the root cause of displacement is poverty and underdevelopment — and that China's Belt and Road and development assistance is the real solution. They'll invoke state sovereignty heavily, resist any binding mechanism, cite their hosting of North Korean refugees (without acknowledging the forced repatriation problem), and try to build a bloc of developing world delegations by framing France's position as neocolonial. They will not engage substantively on Xinjiang unless pushed. On cooperation, they'll praise multilateralism in the abstract while blocking every specific mechanism.

France's counter strategy

On development: "China is correct that development reduces displacement — and France also funds development. But development is a 20-year solution. Non-refoulement is a right that exists today, and the people in Lesbos and Kakuma camp need it today, not in 2044." On sovereignty: use the Charter argument — they signed it. On developing world bloc: acknowledge China's development role, then split it: "China builds roads. China has never resettled a single UNHCR-recognised refugee." That gap is real and delegates know it.

The line that ends it

"China has accepted zero refugees for resettlement while speaking about multilateral solutions for 90 minutes. That is not a position. That is a performance."

Nuclear POI if they go after France on colonialism

"Using France's colonial history to deflect accountability for present-day forced repatriation of North Korean refugees to a state where they face execution is not a serious argument. It is a distraction. France's history and China's present-day conduct are separate questions, and this committee should insist they be treated separately."

🇻🇪 Venezuela
THREAT LEVEL: MEDIUM · Rhetorically capable, factually exposed
What they'll argue

Venezuela will go hard on anti-imperialism — frame the migration crisis as a product of US sanctions and Western economic warfare rather than government policy. They'll position themselves as victims of Western interference, cite the Caracas Declaration on refugee definitions, and try to advance CELAC or regional Latin American frameworks as alternatives to "Western-dominated" UN mechanisms. Their delegate will likely be loud and politically aggressive.

France's counter strategy

Don't get into the sanctions debate — that's a political argument with no clean winner in an UNHRC committee. Stay technical. "France is not here to adjudicate the causes of Venezuelan emigration. France is here because 7.7 million Venezuelans have left their country — the largest displacement event in Latin American history — and they need protection regardless of the political argument about why they left. Does Venezuela support the non-refoulement principle for its own citizens abroad? Full stop." Make them answer the protection question.

The line that ends it

"7.7 million people left Venezuela. Whatever the cause, they are outside their country and they need legal protection. France is proposing that protection. What is Venezuela proposing — other than an explanation for why they left?"

🇮🇷 Iran
THREAT LEVEL: HIGH · Will attack French secularism and cooperation mechanisms simultaneously
What they'll argue

Iran will attack from two angles. First, they'll use their genuine refugee-hosting record (3.4 million Afghans) as a shield to establish credibility — and it's real, so don't dismiss it. Second, they'll use that credibility to then attack binding international mechanisms as Western impositions, question UNHCR's neutrality, and invoke Islamic solidarity as a counter-framework. On cooperation, they'll argue the UN human rights system is politicised and selectively enforced against Muslim-majority states. They may also attack France's laïcité as a form of religious discrimination in a displacement-adjacent argument.

France's counter strategy

Acknowledge the Afghan hosting record explicitly — it's real and you'll lose credibility if you ignore it. "France acknowledges Iran hosts 3.4 million Afghan refugees and this represents a genuine burden that the international community has not adequately supported." Then pivot: "But hosting refugees and providing them with legal rights are two different things. Iranian policy denies Afghan refugees documentation, work rights, and education access in many cases. France is proposing a framework that would actually support Iran's position by providing international funding for host states — Iran has a material interest in supporting it." On laïcité: "France's domestic secularism is a constitutional arrangement that applies equally to all religions. It is not a basis for displacement and it is not what is being discussed in this chamber."

The line that ends it

"Iran hosts 3.4 million Afghans without documentation or work rights. France is proposing international funding and a legal framework that would benefit those exact people. If Iran opposes this proposal, Iran should explain to this committee why."

Hard Questions You'll Get — and How to Answer Them
Q: France has pushed back migrants in the Mediterranean. Isn't this hypocritical?
France acknowledges that European border operations have not always been consistent with the Convention's non-refoulement standard. France is naming that — not defending it. France's position in this committee is precisely that the current system, including France's own practice, needs binding reform. The proposal France is advancing would constrain France as much as any other state. That is not hypocrisy. That is the difference between acknowledging a problem and being the problem.
Q: France's colonial history disqualifies it from lecturing others on rights.
France's colonial history is a fact and France does not dispute it. It is also irrelevant to whether the non-refoulement principle protects refugees today, whether climate displacement is real, and whether UNHCR should be funded adequately. Historical guilt is not a logical argument against specific policy proposals. If the delegation believes colonial history is relevant, France invites it to explain which specific clause in this working paper is invalidated by it.
Q: Binding mechanisms infringe on state sovereignty.
Every state in this room signed the UN Charter. Article 55 and 56 of that Charter create binding human rights obligations. Every state also signed the 1951 Convention — a binding treaty with binding obligations. The question is not whether binding obligations are acceptable in principle, since every member state has already answered yes to that. The question is whether we enforce the ones we've already agreed to. France's position is yes.
Q: Why should developing states trust a Western-led framework?
France's proposal specifically benefits developing host states — Turkey, Uganda, Lebanon, Bangladesh — through mandatory burden-sharing and direct financial transfers. The current voluntary system benefits wealthy states who can opt out. France is proposing to make wealthy states, including France, pay whether they want to or not. If that is a Western framework, it is a strange one. France would also point out that the Kampala Convention — an African instrument — is explicitly named in this working paper as the model for global IDP reform.
